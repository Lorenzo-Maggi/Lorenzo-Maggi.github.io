This project represents the synthesis of my academic journey (BSc and MSc) and my passion for translational research. The system was designed to bridge the gap between engineering and clinical organ preservation.

### Project Overview
The core challenge was to create a controlled environment capable of simulating physiological conditions for organs kept outside the body. This involved the precise regulation of temperature, pressure, and nutrient flow to prevent tissue degradation.

### Technical Implementation
*   **Mechanical Design:** Developed the fluidic circuit and organ chamber using **SolidWorks** for 3D modeling and structural integrity.
*   **Electronics & Control:** Implemented an **Arduino-based** control system for real-time monitoring of physiological parameters.
*   **Prototyping (Maker Approach):** 
    *   Manufactured custom components using **FDM 3D printing**.
    *   Performed precision **soldering (SMD)** for custom sensor interfaces.
    *   Conducted rigorous **sensor calibration** to ensure data accuracy during long-term perfusion tests.

### Key Features
*   **Normothermic Regulation:** Maintains a stable 37°C environment.
*   **Pulsatile Flow Simulation:** Mimics natural cardiac output for better tissue oxygenation.
*   **Data Logging:** Real-time feedback of pressure and flow rates via a custom-coded interface.

> **Thesis Highlight:** This work provided the foundation for my subsequent PhD research in surgical robotics and ex-vivo experimentation, focusing on a strong maker-oriented methodology.
